# ICSAC Review Rubric: AI Provenance Signal

This document is a checklist for catching submissions whose prose carries signals of low-engagement provenance — template-shaped, evidentially thin, or constructed from fabricated scaffolding — whether generated by a model, a human writing on autopilot, or anything in between. Any submission triggering multiple flags below should receive an AI provenance signal score of 1. Per the calibration rubric, a score of 1 on AI Provenance Signal routes the submission to the revise-and-resubmit path — the author receives the panel's specific concerns and may resubmit a revised version.

The dimension does not measure whether AI tooling was used. Disclosed AI-assisted writing that produces specific, verifiable, substantively engaged work scores cleanly. The dimension measures whether the prose appears to have come from a process that engaged with the subject matter at all — model, human, or any combination.

## Red Flags

### Abstract and Framing

- **Generic abstract**: The abstract could describe any paper in the field. It contains no specific claims, no concrete results, and no identifiable contribution. Test: could this abstract be swapped onto a different paper without anyone noticing?
- **Excessive hedging with no concrete claims**: The submission is entirely composed of qualifiers ("may," "could," "potentially," "it is possible that") without ever committing to a specific finding, result, or position.

> **Worked example.** An abstract that opens "This paper explores the intersection of complex systems and emergent dynamics in nonlinear settings" without ever naming the system, the metric, or the result — generic enough to fit on any complexity paper from the last twenty years — fails this test even if the rest of the paper is reasonable. The abstract is the contract; an empty contract is a flag.

### Citations and References

- **Fabricated citations**: DOIs that do not resolve, author names that do not appear in any publication database, or journal names that do not exist. Even one fabricated citation is grounds for a provenance score of 1 on its own — the rest of the rubric does not need to be invoked.
- **Citation stuffing**: References that are real publications but have no meaningful connection to the submission's content. The reference list exists to create an appearance of scholarship rather than to situate the work.

### Methodology

- **Circular reasoning disguised as methodology**: The submission defines a measure, applies it, and then claims the results validate the measure -- without independent verification or external ground truth.
- **Methodology section that describes no actual method**: The section uses methodological language ("we analyzed," "we computed," "we evaluated") but never specifies what was actually done, on what data, or with what tools.

> **Worked example.** "We evaluated the framework against a range of benchmarks and observed consistent improvement" — with no benchmark named, no baseline reported, and no code or data trail — is a methodology shape, not a methodology. The same paragraph with "on CIFAR-10 vs. ResNet-50, +2.3% top-1, code at github.com/..." is a methodology. The difference is verifiability: a reader can either follow the trail or they cannot.

### Writing Quality

- **Padded word count with no substance**: Paragraphs that restate the same point in different words, filler sentences that add no information, or lengthy introductions that never arrive at a contribution.
- **Perfect grammar but zero domain expertise signals**: The writing is fluent and error-free but contains no specialized terminology, no engagement with known open problems, and no evidence the author has read the literature they cite.

### Structural Signals

- **Uniform section lengths**: Every section is suspiciously similar in length, suggesting template-based generation rather than organic writing driven by content.
- **No engagement with counterarguments or alternative explanations**: The submission presents its claims as though no competing perspectives exist. Genuine researchers in complexity science are aware of debates in their subfield.
- **Figures or tables that do not match the text**: Captions describe results not present in the figure, or figures are generic stock visualizations unrelated to the claimed analysis.

## Reviewer Guidance

No single flag forces a low score on its own — fabricated citations are the one exception, and they warrant a provenance score of 1 by themselves. Otherwise the score reflects the overall pattern. A submission with one minor flag and otherwise solid content should not be penalized heavily. A submission with three or more flags, particularly fabricated citations or a vacuous methodology section, should receive a provenance score of 1 — which routes to revise-and-resubmit so the author can address the panel's specific concerns.

When flagging a provenance signal, cite the specific passages or references that triggered the concern. Do not make vague accusations. A flag without a citation is itself a low-engagement review and will be caught by the review-quality-control audit.
